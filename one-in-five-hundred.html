<  One in Five Hundred  />

Project

Brief

If the infographic is not created to serve the needs of the audience, it is useless, although visually pleasing. To create an infographic that effectively communicates to the audience, designers need to identify communication issues, analyze the audience and the context, and then select the appropriate style. Conduct comparative critique of information design and visualization works using contextual criteria and develop alternative solutions.

Redesign

For this project, I chose to redesign a data visualization produced by the Washington Post for an article documenting a landmark number of COVID-19 deaths in September of 2021.

Many of the visualizations I have seen around death and COVID-19 are plain and feel as if they could be published in a scientific journal. The collective hardship that we've all experienced is not visualized in the Washington Post visualizations. The drain and grief specific to certain communities in the United States also aren't conveyed.

Data

I used data from the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). I cleaned and transformed the data in R by grouping the data by age group and rolling up the provisional death counts. Next, I computed the average rate at which people died over one hour throughout the pandemic.

Research

In the second week of the project, I identified what I thought wasn't working well in the Washington Post's visualization of 1 in 500 deaths. I felt that the Washington Post's visualization lacked narrative and did not focus on the disproportionate effects of the pandemic. I researched other visualizations which conveyed emotion. The first visualization is an animated visualization from Perioscope. A unique part of the Periscope visualization is the use of tempo and speed.

Sketches

In the initial sketch, I wanted to show points moving across a page. The arcs would fall at different points indicating a higher or lower rate of death by age group.

I chose a dark background because of the seriousness of the topic. However, I used color to represent each different age group. As the points move across a page they transition from color to a muted gray, signifying death.
